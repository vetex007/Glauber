GLISSANDO 3: Monte Carlo Glauber Simulations

GLISSANDO 3 is a modern Monte Carlo implementation of the Glauber model, designed to simulate the initial geometric configuration of relativistic nuclear collisions. It provides a bridge between the theoretical framework of Glauber modeling and the practical requirements of heavy-ion data analysis.

The primary purpose of GLISSANDO 3 is to generate event-by-event collision geometries, including fluctuations in nucleon positions and interaction patterns, and to calculate geometric observables relevant for experimental and phenomenological studies.

Why Monte Carlo Glauber?

While the optical Glauber model yields accurate average quantities, it cannot describe event-by-event fluctuations that play a crucial role in modern heavy-ion physics. Observables such as anisotropic flow coefficients are sensitive to fluctuations in the initial geometry.

GLISSANDO 3 addresses this limitation by explicitly sampling individual nucleons and simulating collisions on an event-by-event basis, thereby capturing realistic fluctuations in:

  • Number of participating nucleons
  • Number of binary collisions
  • Spatial eccentricities of the overlap region
  • Energy or entropy deposition profiles

Event Generation Workflow

Each GLISSANDO 3 event represents a single nuclear collision with a specific geometric configuration. The simulation proceeds through the following steps:

  1. Sampling nucleon positions inside each nucleus
  2. Selection of a random impact parameter
  3. Determination of nucleon–nucleon collisions
  4. Identification of participant and spectator nucleons
  5. Calculation of geometric observables

Nuclear Geometry and Input Parameters

GLISSANDO 3 models each nucleus as a collection of nucleons distributed according to realistic nuclear density profiles. For heavy nuclei, the Woods–Saxon distribution is used, with parameters taken from experimental measurements.

  • R — nuclear radius
  • a — surface diffuseness
  • A — mass number of the nucleus
  • σNNinel — inelastic nucleon–nucleon cross section

The inelastic nucleon–nucleon cross section is energy dependent and serves as a crucial external input to the simulation.

Collision Criterion

Two nucleons from different nuclei are assumed to collide if their transverse separation satisfies the geometric condition:

dT ≤ √(σNNinel / π)

This “black-disk” approximation provides a simple and efficient way to implement nucleon–nucleon interactions while preserving the correct average collision rates.

Participants, Binary Collisions, and Centrality

After determining all nucleon–nucleon interactions in an event, GLISSANDO 3 identifies:

  • Participants (Npart): nucleons that interact at least once
  • Binary collisions (Ncoll): total number of NN interactions

These quantities form the basis for centrality determination. Events with large Npart correspond to central collisions, while small values indicate peripheral collisions.

Initial-State Geometry and Eccentricities

A key strength of GLISSANDO 3 is its ability to calculate spatial eccentricities of the initial overlap region. These eccentricities quantify the shape of the participant zone and play a central role in the development of collective flow.

The n-th order eccentricity is defined as:

εn = \frac{\sqrt{⟨rn cos(nφ)⟩² + ⟨rn sin(nφ)⟩²}} {⟨rn⟩}

Event-by-event fluctuations in εn provide the initial conditions necessary to explain observed flow harmonics in heavy-ion collisions.

Output and Analysis

GLISSANDO 3 produces output files containing both event-level and ensemble-averaged observables. These outputs are typically analyzed using ROOT to construct distributions of:

  • Npart and Ncoll
  • Impact parameter distributions
  • Participant densities in the transverse plane
  • Eccentricities εn

These quantities serve as essential inputs for hydrodynamic models and provide a baseline for interpreting experimental data.
