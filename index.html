Introduction

Ultra-relativistic heavy-ion collisions provide a unique laboratory for studying strongly interacting matter under extreme conditions of temperature and energy density. At sufficiently high energies, Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD) predicts the formation of a deconfined state of quarks and gluons, commonly referred to as the Quarkâ€“Gluon Plasma (QGP).

Experiments at modern particle accelerators collide heavy nuclei at ultra-relativistic energies, creating systems that briefly reach conditions similar to those believed to have existed in the early universe. The properties of the produced medium are inferred indirectly through final-state observables such as particle multiplicities, collective flow, and quarkonium production.

A precise understanding of the initial geometry of nuclear collisions is essential for interpreting these observables. Since the early stages of the collision are not directly accessible experimentally, theoretical models play a crucial role. Among these, the Glauber model has become a standard framework for estimating the collision geometry and the number of interacting nucleons.

In this documentation, we present a detailed discussion of the Glauber model, its historical development, mathematical formulation, and its practical implementation using the Monte Carlo event generator GLISSANDO 3. The aim is to provide a clear and structured reference for understanding Glauber-based simulations in heavy-ion physics.
