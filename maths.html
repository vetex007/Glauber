Mathematical Framework of the Glauber Model

The Glauber model provides a quantitative framework to describe the geometry of high-energy nuclear collisions. Its goal is not to model the full dynamics of the strong interaction, but rather to determine geometric quantities that characterize the initial state of the collision.

In this section, we present the mathematical foundations of the Glauber model, following the standard formulation used in modern heavy-ion analyses :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}.

Basic Physical Assumptions

  • Nuclei consist of nucleons distributed according to measured density profiles
  • Nucleons travel along straight-line trajectories at ultra-relativistic energies
  • Binary nucleon–nucleon collisions occur probabilistically
  • The inelastic nucleon–nucleon cross section σNNinel is an external input

These assumptions allow a separation of collision geometry from microscopic particle production dynamics.

---

Nuclear Density Distribution

For medium and heavy nuclei, the spatial distribution of nucleons is commonly parameterized using the Woods–Saxon (Fermi) distribution:

ρ(r) = ρ0 / [1 + exp((r − R)/a)]

Here, R is the nuclear radius, a is the surface diffuseness parameter, and ρ0 is the central density. This form reproduces electron–nucleus scattering data and provides realistic nuclear geometries :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}.

::contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3} ---

Nuclear Thickness Function

Since collisions are treated in the transverse plane, the three-dimensional density is integrated along the beam direction to define the nuclear thickness function:

TA(s) = ∫ ρA(s, z) dz

The thickness function represents the probability density of finding a nucleon at a given transverse position s. It has dimensions of inverse area and plays a central role in Glauber calculations.

::contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4} ---

Nuclear Overlap Function

For a collision between nuclei A and B at impact parameter b, the overlap function is defined as:

TAB(b) = ∫ TA(s) TB(s − b) d²s

The overlap function quantifies the effective transverse area where nucleons from the two nuclei can interact. It provides the geometric foundation for calculating collision probabilities.

---

Inelastic Nucleus–Nucleus Cross Section

Within the optical Glauber approximation, the probability for at least one inelastic nucleon–nucleon interaction at impact parameter b is:

Pinel(b) = 1 − [1 − σNNinel TAB(b)]AB

Integrating over all impact parameters yields the total inelastic nucleus–nucleus cross section:

σABinel = ∫ 2πb db · Pinel(b)

---

Number of Binary Collisions (Ncoll)

The average number of binary nucleon–nucleon collisions at a given impact parameter is given by:

Ncoll(b) = AB · TAB(b) · σNNinel

This quantity is essential for scaling hard processes such as jet and heavy quark production.

---

Number of Participating Nucleons (Npart)

A nucleon is considered a participant if it undergoes at least one inelastic collision. The number of participants is given by:

Npart(b) = A ∫ TA(s) [1 − (1 − σNNinel TB(s − b))B] d²s + B ∫ TB(s − b) [1 − (1 − σNNinel TA(s))A] d²s

Npart provides a natural measure of collision centrality and is strongly correlated with particle multiplicity.

::contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5} ---

Limitations of the Optical Approximation

The optical Glauber model assumes smooth nuclear densities and neglects event-by-event fluctuations. While it provides accurate average values of Npart and Ncoll, it cannot describe fluctuations in geometry or eccentricity.

These limitations motivate the Monte Carlo Glauber approach, where nucleons are treated as discrete objects and collisions are simulated event by event :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}.
