Mathematical Framework of the Glauber Model

In the Glauber model, atomic nuclei are treated as collections of nucleons whose spatial distribution follows a nuclear density profile. For medium and heavy nuclei, the Woods–Saxon distribution is commonly employed:

ρ(r) = ρ0 / [1 + exp((r − R)/a)]

Here, R represents the nuclear radius, a is the surface diffuseness parameter, and ρ0 is the central nuclear density. This form reflects an approximately constant density in the nuclear interior with a smooth falloff at the surface.

At ultra-relativistic energies, nucleons are assumed to travel along straight-line trajectories. A nucleon–nucleon collision occurs when the transverse distance between two nucleons from different nuclei satisfies:

dT ≤ √(σNN / π)

where σNN denotes the inelastic nucleon–nucleon cross section at the given collision energy. This geometric criterion determines whether an inelastic interaction takes place.

The number of participating nucleons, denoted as Npart, corresponds to nucleons that undergo at least one inelastic collision. The number of binary collisions, Ncoll, counts the total number of individual nucleon–nucleon interactions.

These quantities form the basis for centrality determination and scaling relations in heavy-ion collisions and serve as essential inputs to hydrodynamic and transport models.
